COMPREHENSIVE SEPSIS MANAGEMENT GUIDELINES - 2023 EDITION
====================================================================

1. DEFINITION AND EPIDEMIOLOGY

Sepsis is defined as life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by dysregulated host response to infection. 
It represents a leading cause of morbidity and mortality in hospitals worldwide, affecting over 49 million 
people annually. Septic shock occurs when sepsis is accompanied by circulatory, cellular, and metabolic 
dysfunction, characterized by sustained hypotension requiring vasopressors to maintain mean arterial 
pressure (MAP) ≥ 65 mmHg and elevated lactate (≥ 2 mmol/L) despite adequate fluid resuscitation.

The incidence of sepsis continues to rise, with mortality rates ranging from 15-40% depending on severity 
and organ involvement. Risk factors include age > 60 years, immunocompromise, recent surgery, indwelling 
catheters, and chronic comorbidities such as diabetes mellitus, chronic kidney disease, and malignancy.

2. EARLY RECOGNITION AND SCREENING

2.1 Clinical Suspicion
Healthcare providers should maintain high clinical suspicion for sepsis in patients presenting with:
- Acute fever (temperature > 38.3°C or < 36°C) with altered mentation
- Tachycardia (heart rate > 100 bpm) with respiratory distress
- New onset hypotension or signs of poor perfusion
- Acute change in mental status or confusion
- Severe infection source suspected or confirmed

2.2 Screening Tools

qSOFA Score (Quick Sequential Organ Failure Assessment):
- Altered mental status (1 point)
- Respiratory rate ≥ 22 (1 point)
- Systolic blood pressure ≤ 100 mmHg (1 point)
Score ≥ 2 suggests possible sepsis with mortality risk 3-25%

NEWS2 Score (National Early Warning Score 2):
- Respiratory rate: 20-24 (1 point), ≥ 25 (3 points)
- Oxygen saturation: 94-95% (1 point), < 92% (3 points)
- Temperature: 36-36.9°C (1 point), < 36°C or ≥ 39.1°C (2 points)
- Systolic BP: 101-110 (1 point), ≤ 90 (3 points)
- Heart rate: 101-110 (1 point), > 110 (2 points), < 40 (3 points)
- AVPU: Alert (0), Verbal (3), Pain (3), Unresponsive (3)
Score ≥ 5 requires urgent assessment

SOFA Score (Sequential Organ Failure Assessment):
Assess organ function in six systems (max 24 points):
- Respiratory (PaO2/FiO2 ratio)
- Coagulation (platelet count)
- Hepatic (bilirubin level)
- Cardiovascular (vasopressor requirement)
- Central nervous system (Glasgow Coma Scale)
- Renal (creatinine or urine output)
Score ≥ 2 associated with increased mortality

3. DIAGNOSTIC STUDIES

3.1 Microbiology
- Blood cultures: Obtain at least 2 sets (one aerobic, one anaerobic) from different sites before antibiotics
- Culture timing: Ideally 30 minutes before antibiotic administration, but maximum delay should be < 3 hours
- Site-specific cultures: Include sputum, urine, wound, CSF, or other body fluids based on clinical suspicion
- Gram stain: Consider for rapid identification in specific samples

3.2 Laboratory Investigations
Complete Blood Count:
- White blood cell count: Elevation (> 12,000) or depression (< 4,000) both suggest sepsis
- Platelet count: Thrombocytopenia (< 100,000) indicates increased severity and organ dysfunction
- Differential: Left shift with increased band forms

Metabolic Panel:
- Electrolyte abnormalities: Hyponatremia, hyperkalemia common in severe sepsis
- Kidney function: Creatinine, BUN - assess for acute kidney injury
- Liver function: Bilirubin, transaminases - evaluate hepatic dysfunction

Coagulation Studies:
- PT/INR and aPTT: Prolongation may indicate DIC
- Fibrinogen: Decreased in advanced DIC
- D-dimer: Markedly elevated in sepsis-induced coagulopathy

3.3 Lactate and Perfusion Markers
- Initial lactate: Obtain at presentation; level identifies tissue hypoperfusion
- Lactate > 4 mmol/L: Associated with significantly increased mortality
- Serial lactate: Measurement at 3 and 6 hours helps assess response to resuscitation
- Lactate clearance: < 10% reduction in 6 hours indicates poor prognosis
- Base deficit: Negative base deficit indicates metabolic acidosis from tissue hypoperfusion

3.4 Imaging Studies
- Chest X-ray: Evaluate for pneumonia, ARDS, pulmonary edema
- CT imaging: Consider CT abdomen/pelvis if source is abdominal but not clinically obvious
- Ultrasound: Bedside evaluation for abdominal fluid, ventricular function assessment
- Blood cultures should always precede imaging delays

4. ANTIBIOTIC THERAPY

4.1 Timing and Selection
- Empiric antibiotics MUST be administered within 1 hour of recognition (3 hours if admitted from emergency department)
- Delay in antibiotics increases mortality by approximately 8% for each hour of delay
- Antibiotic selection depends on:
  * Site of infection
  * Local resistance patterns
  * Patient risk factors for resistant organisms
  * Renal and hepatic function

4.2 Empiric Regimens (Common Scenarios)

Community-Acquired Infection:
- Ceftriaxone 1-2 g IV Q12H + Azithromycin or Fluoroquinolone
- For Pseudomonas risk: Add Fluoroquinolone or Aminoglycoside

Hospital-Acquired Infection:
- Piperacillin-Tazobactam 4.5 g IV Q6H OR Cephalosporin (4th generation)
- For MRSA risk: Add Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg Q8-12H

Intra-Abdominal Source:
- Piperacillin-Tazobactam OR Cephalosporin + Anaerobe coverage
- Consider Metronidazole if anaerobic coverage needed

CNS Source (Meningitis):
- Ceftriaxone 2 g IV Q4H + Vancomycin + Ampicillin (for Listeria coverage if > 50 years or immunocompromised)
- Add Dexamethasone 10 mg IV Q6H x 4 days

4.3 De-escalation
- Narrow spectrum after organism identification and susceptibilities known (typically 48-72 hours)
- Continue therapy for appropriate duration based on source (typically 7-14 days)
- Repeat cultures to document sterilization when feasible

5. FLUID RESUSCITATION

5.1 Initial Fluid Management
- Goal: Restore circulating volume to normalize perfusion and decrease lactate
- Crystalloid fluid of choice: Lactated Ringer's or Normal Saline
- Fluid bolus protocol:
  * 30 mL/kg over first 3 hours for hypotension (SBP < 100 mmHg) OR lactate > 4 mmol/L
  * Example: 70 kg patient = 2100 mL rapid infusion

5.2 Reassessment and Ongoing Management
- Measure response: Decrease in lactate, improvement in mental status, reversal of signs of poor perfusion
- Reassess need for ongoing fluid administration every 1-2 hours initially
- Use dynamic measures of fluid responsiveness:
  * Passive leg raise test
  * Pulse pressure variation (if mechanically ventilated)
  * Stroke volume variation

5.3 Fluid Overload Recognition
- Discontinue bolus fluids when:
  * Clinical improvement achieved
  * Signs of fluid overload evident (crackles, peripheral edema, increased JVP)
  * CVP > 12 mmHg or PCWP > 18 mmHg
- Excessive fluid associated with poor outcomes including ARDS and mortality

6. VASOPRESSOR SUPPORT

6.1 Indications for Vasopressor Initiation
- Persistent hypotension (SBP < 90 mmHg or MAP < 65 mmHg) despite fluid resuscitation
- Persistent lactate elevation despite adequate fluid support
- Typically initiated during or immediately after completion of initial fluid bolus

6.2 Agent Selection and Dosing

Norepinephrine (First-Line Agent):
- Starting dose: 0.01-0.05 mcg/kg/min IV
- Titrate to MAP ≥ 65 mmHg (usual range 0.1-0.5 mcg/kg/min)
- Preferred first vasopressor due to balanced alpha and beta effects
- Results in improved outcomes compared to dopamine

Dopamine (If Norepinephrine Unavailable):
- Starting dose: 5 mcg/kg/min IV
- Titrate to desired MAP (usual range 5-20 mcg/kg/min)
- Higher risk of tachycardia and arrhythmias compared to norepinephrine
- Use only if norepinephrine contraindicated or unavailable

Epinephrine (When Additional Agents Needed):
- Indicated if norepinephrine insufficient
- Starting dose: 0.01 mcg/kg/min, titrate as needed
- Increased risk of tachycardia and lactic acidosis

Vasopressin (Adjunctive Agent):
- Low-dose vasopressin 0.04 units/min (fixed dose, not titrated)
- Consider adding to norepinephrine if inadequate response
- No mortality benefit as monotherapy

6.3 Monitoring During Vasopressor Support
- Continuous blood pressure monitoring preferred via arterial catheter
- Assess perfusion: Urine output, mental status, extremity perfusion, lactate
- Monitor for digitalis toxicity if patient on digoxin
- Avoid extravasation through central line; peripheral administration acceptable for short term with close monitoring

7. SOURCE CONTROL

7.1 General Principles
- Source control is essential for sepsis management alongside antibiotics and supportive care
- Operative interventions must be performed expeditiously for surgically drainable collections
- Timing of intervention typically within 12-24 hours of diagnosis

7.2 Source Control Measures by Site

Intra-Abdominal:
- Appendicitis: Appendectomy
- Perforated viscus: Repair and source control
- Obstruction with perforation: Resection and anastomosis or colostomy
- Abscess: Percutaneous or surgical drainage

Skin and Soft Tissue:
- Necrotizing fasciitis: Immediate surgical debridement, repeat every 24-48 hours as needed
- Cellulitis: IV antibiotics (may not require drainage unless abscess)
- Abscesses: Incision and drainage

Respiratory:
- Pneumonia: Usually medical management; consider bronchoscopy if obstruction
- Pleural effusion/empyema: Drainage if infected

Urinary:
- Obstructed infected urinary system: Urgent drainage (catheter, stent, or percutaneous)
- Pyelonephritis: Usually responds to antibiotics alone

Intravascular:
- Infected central lines: Remove and replace
- Endocarditis: Optimize antibiotics; surgery for large vegetations, perforations, or prosthetic involvement

7.3 Infection Prevention
- Maintain aseptic techniques for procedures
- Remove unnecessary catheters promptly
- Avoid manipulation of necrotic or gangrenous tissue
- Consider vaccination when sepsis resolves

8. SUPPORTIVE CARE

8.1 Respiratory Support
Indications for Mechanical Ventilation:
- Respiratory failure (RR > 35, severe distress, altered mental status limiting airway protection)
- Severe hypoxemia (PaO2 < 60 mmHg on high-flow oxygen)
- ARDS development

Low Tidal Volume Strategy:
- Target 6-8 mL/kg predicted body weight
- Reduces barotrauma and improves outcomes
- PEEP at least 5 cm H2O; increase based on FiO2 requirements

8.2 Renal Support
- Avoid nephrotoxic medications (NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors)
- Maintain adequate perfusion pressure
- Continue renal replacement therapy for acute kidney injury with standard indications
- Creatinine normalization may lag hemodynamic recovery

8.3 Glucose Control
- Maintain target glucose 140-180 mg/dL (avoid hypoglycemia < 70 mg/dL)
- Use insulin infusion for persistent hyperglycemia
- Transition to subcutaneous insulin once stabilized and able to eat

8.4 Stress Ulcer Prophylaxis
- Consider H2-blocker or PPI for patients with risk factors:
  * Mechanical ventilation
  * Coagulopathy
  * High-dose steroids
- Target prophylaxis in high-risk patients

8.5 DVT Prophylaxis
- Sequential compression devices for all ICU patients
- Pharmacologic prophylaxis (enoxaparin 40 mg SC daily) unless contraindicated by active bleeding

9. NOT RECOMMENDED

- Activated Protein C (drotrecogin alfa): No mortality benefit, increased bleeding risk
- Low-dose corticosteroids: Only for refractory hypotension after adequate fluid and vasopressor support
- Tight glycemic control (target 80-110): Increased hypoglycemia risk without benefit
- Antimotility agents in sepsis: Increased risk of toxic megacolon
- Routine immunoglobulin infusions
- High-frequency oscillatory ventilation as first-line ventilation strategy

10. PROGNOSIS AND OUTCOMES

Factors Associated with Poor Prognosis:
- Delayed recognition and treatment
- Septic shock presentation
- Higher SOFA scores at admission
- Immunocompromise
- Advanced age
- Multiple comorbidities
- Delayed source control

Factors Associated with Recovery:
- Rapid recognition and appropriate antibiotics
- Successful source control
- Adequate fluid resuscitation
- Appropriate vasopressor use
- Young age and limited comorbidities
- ICU monitoring and support

Mortality Rates:
- Sepsis (no shock): 15-20%
- Septic shock: 35-55%
- With adequate management: Up to 50% reduction in mortality

Follow-up and Recovery:
- Post-sepsis syndrome: Many survivors experience prolonged cognitive impairment, fatigue, and weakness
- Rehabilitation assessment after discharge
- Psychological support for PTSD symptoms
- Reassess medications initially started for sepsis management (particularly corticosteroids, vasopressors)
- Monitor for secondary infections during recovery

11. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

11.1 Immunocompromised Patients
- Include opportunistic pathogens in differential
- Broader empiric coverage initially
- Earlier source control if fungal suspected
- Prolonged antibiotic course sometimes needed

11.2 Pregnant Patients
- Similar treatment as non-pregnant
- Avoid certain antibiotics (fluoroquinolones, tetracyclines)
- Early delivery consideration if maternal/fetal compromise
- Thromboprophylaxis important due to increased VTE risk

11.3 Elderly Patients
- Similar management principles apply
- May present with atypical symptoms
- Careful fluid management due to comorbidities
- Functional status considerations in goals of care discussion

12. QUALITY METRICS AND AUDIT

Hospital sepsis protocol compliance should be monitored:
- Percentage of patients with blood cultures obtained before antibiotics
- Time to antibiotics from recognition (goal < 1 hour)
- Percentage of patients with lactate measurement
- Percentage of patients with appropriate imaging before treatment delays
- ICU admission rate and length of stay
- Hospital mortality rate
- 30-day readmission rate for sepsis
