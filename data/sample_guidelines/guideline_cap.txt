====================================================================
COMMUNITY-ACQUIRED PNEUMONIA (CAP) MANAGEMENT GUIDELINES - 2023
====================================================================

1. DEFINITION AND EPIDEMIOLOGY

1.1 Definition
Community-acquired pneumonia is defined as infection of the lung parenchyma associated with 
acute illness (symptoms < 2 weeks) in ambulatory patients who do not meet criteria for 
hospitalization or who are hospitalized but infection did not begin until after 48 hours of hospitalization.

1.2 Epidemiology
Incidence: 4-7 million cases annually in the United States
Hospitalization rate: 900,000-1,000,000 per year
Mortality: 1-4% in outpatients, 5-10% in hospitalized patients, 30-40% in ICU patients
Cost: Over $15 billion annually in direct and indirect costs
Seasonality: More common in winter months, particularly during influenza season

1.3 Risk Factors and Severity Predictors
High-Risk Comorbidities:
- Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)
- Asthma
- Chronic heart failure
- Chronic kidney disease
- Diabetes mellitus
- Immunosuppression (HIV, malignancy, immunosuppressive therapy)
- Chronic liver disease
- Structural lung disease (bronchiectasis, prior tuberculosis)

Age-Related Risk:
- Age ≥ 65 years associated with worse outcomes
- Children < 5 years at increased risk

Social/Behavioral Risk:
- Active smoking
- Excessive alcohol use
- Drug use (especially with impaired consciousness)
- Recent hospitalization or antibiotic use
- Living in crowded conditions

2. CLINICAL PRESENTATION AND DIAGNOSIS

2.1 Symptoms
Classic Presentation:
- Cough (productive or nonproductive): Present in 70-80% of cases
- Fever (temperature > 38°C): Present in 60-70%
- Dyspnea: Present in 50-75%
- Pleuritic chest pain: Present in 30-40%
- Sputum character: May be mucoid, purulent, or blood-tinged

Atypical Presentations:
- Elderly patients may present with altered mental status without fever
- Immunocompromised patients may have minimal symptoms
- Viral prodrome preceding bacterial infection
- GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea)
- Myalgias and arthralgias

2.2 Physical Examination Findings
Respiratory Findings:
- Tachypnea (respiratory rate > 30 suggests severe disease)
- Hypoxemia (oxygen saturation < 92% concerning)
- Crackles/rales on auscultation (consolidation)
- Bronchial breath sounds (consolidation)
- Wheezes (airway obstruction)
- Dullness to percussion (pleural effusion)
- Egophony (spoken words sound nasal when auscultated)

Cardiovascular Findings:
- Tachycardia (> 100 bpm associated with worse prognosis)
- Hypotension (systolic BP < 90 mmHg component of severe sepsis)
- Signs of right heart strain in severe cases

General:
- Levels of consciousness/confusion
- Signs of dehydration
- Cyanosis

2.3 Diagnostic Imaging
Chest X-ray:
- Gold standard for pneumonia diagnosis
- Infiltrate patterns: Lobar (typical bacteria), bronchial (atypical), interstitial (viral, fungal)
- Extent of disease: Single vs multiple segments
- Complications: Pleural effusion, empyema, pneumothorax
- Cavitation: Consider TB or fungal disease
- Response to therapy at follow-up

CT Chest:
- High-resolution CT for atypical presentations
- Evaluation of complications (empyema, lung abscess)
- Assessment for underlying malignancy
- Not routinely indicated but useful in severe or worsening cases

2.4 Microbiology and Culture
Sputum Culture:
- Obtain before antibiotics when possible
- Interpret based on Gram stain results
- Pure growth of organism more useful than mixed flora
- Important for de-escalation of therapy

Blood Cultures:
- Obtain in hospitalized patients, especially if severe
- Positive in 10-20% of pneumococcal CAP
- Less likely positive in atypical pathogens

Rapid Diagnostic Tests:
- Influenza and RSV testing: Guides antiviral therapy decisions
- Legionella urinary antigen: If legionella suspected
- Streptococcal pneumoniae urinary antigen: Invasive disease indicator

3. PATHOGENIC ORGANISMS AND EPIDEMIOLOGY

3.1 Common Organisms
Streptococcus pneumoniae:
- Most common cause of bacterial CAP (30-40% of cases)
- Risk factors: Age, COPD, asplenia, smoking
- Presentation: Rapid onset, often with pleurisy

Haemophilus influenzae (NTHi):
- Less common in vaccinated populations
- Associated with COPD
- Risk factors: Smoking, male gender

Atypical Organisms (20-30% of CAP):
Mycoplasma pneumoniae: Young age, gradual onset, nonproductive cough
Chlamydia pneumoniae: Older age, associated with atherosclerosis discussion
Legionella pneumophila: Elderly, immunocompromised, contaminated water source
- Presents with high fever, GI symptoms, altered mental status
- Extrapulmonary complications (hepatitis, rhabdomyolysis)

Viral Causes (15-30% of CAP):
- Influenza A/B: Winter months, rapid onset, myalgias
- Rhinovirus: Year-round, often self-limited
- Parainfluenza, coronavirus, adenovirus
- RSV: Winter months, elderly and immunocompromised at high risk

Gram-Negative Bacilli:
- Klebsiella pneumoniae: COPD, diabetes, alcoholism, risk for cavitation
- Pseudomonas aeruginosa: COPD exacerbation, cystic fibrosis, recent hospitalization
- Other Enterobacteriaceae

3.2 Resistant Organisms
Drug-Resistant Streptococcus pneumoniae (DRSP):
- Penicillin non-susceptibility: Increased globally
- High-risk factors: Recent antibiotic use, age > 65
- Higher mortality with delayed appropriate therapy

Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA):
- CAP-MRSA generally less virulent than HA-MRSA
- Risk: Recent hospitalization, MRSA colonization, COPD
- Increasing prevalence in community

Antimicrobial resistance patterns vary by geography and healthcare facility

4. SEVERITY ASSESSMENT AND STRATIFICATION

4.1 Severity Scores

CURB-65 Score (for CAP severity/hospitalization decision):
- Confusion (new acute onset): 1 point
- Urea (serum > 7 mmol/L): 1 point
- Respiratory rate (≥ 30): 1 point
- Blood pressure (SBP ≤ 90 OR DBP ≤ 60): 1 point
- Age (≥ 65): 1 point
Interpretation:
- 0 points: Can treat as outpatient
- 1 point: Consider hospitalization
- 2 points: Likely needs hospitalization
- ≥ 3 points: Severe CAP, consider ICU 
Mortality increases with each point

PSI (Pneumonia Severity Index) Score:
- Incorporates demographics, comorbidities, vital signs, laboratory values
- More complex calculation than CURB-65
- Risk classes I-V correlate with mortality
- Class I: < 0.1% mortality (outpatient)
- Class II-III: Hospitalization versus observation
- Class IV-V: Hospital admission with consideration for ICU

4.2 Markers of Severe Disease
Clinical:
- Respiratory rate ≥ 30 breaths/minute
- Systolic BP < 90 mmHg
- Diastolic BP ≤ 60 mmHg
- Altered mental status/confusion
- Severe renal disease (serum creatinine > 2 mg/dL)
- Severe hepatic disease
- Severe sepsis or septic shock

Radiographic:
- Bilateral infiltrates
- Multilobar involvement
- Rapid progression of infiltrates
- Pleural effusion
- Evidence of cavitation

Laboratory:
- WBC count < 4,000 or > 30,000
- Severe hypoxemia (PaO2 < 60 mmHg)
- Arterial pH < 7.35
- Serum lactate > 2 mmol/L
- Thrombocytopenia (< 50,000)
- High PSI score

5. ANTIBIOTIC THERAPY

5.1 Outpatient (Non-Hospitalized) Treatment

Previously Healthy, No Antibiotics in Past 3 Months:
- Amoxicillin: 1 gram TID × 5-7 days
- Doxycycline: 100 mg BID × 5-7 days
- Macrolide (azithromycin): 500 mg day 1, then 250 mg × 4 days
Note: Respiratory fluoroquinolones highly effective but reserve for resistant pathogens

Risk Factors for Resistance (Comorbidities, Recent Antibiotics):
- Respiratory fluoroquinolone: Levofloxacin 750 mg daily OR Moxifloxacin 400 mg daily × 5 days
- Beta-lactam + macrolide: Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BID + Azithromycin
- Consider local resistance patterns

Clinically Failing Outpatient Therapy:
- Switch to respiratory fluoroquinolone if not already using
- Consider hospitalization
- Evaluate for complications (empyema, abscess)
- Consider atypical or viral etiology

5.2 Hospitalized, Non-Severe CAP (General Ward)

No Risk Factors for Pseudomonas, MRSA:
- Beta-lactam (cephalosporin preferred): Ceftriaxone 1-2 g daily OR Cefotaxime 1-2 g equally
- PLUS macrolide: Azithromycin 500 mg daily
OR monotherapy with respiratory fluoroquinolone if macrolide allergy

With Risk Factors for MRSA (recent hospitalization, IV drug use, COPD):
- Include vancomycin: 15-20 mg/kg Q8-12H (goal trough 15-20 mcg/mL)
- Plus beta-lactam: Ceftriaxone 1-2 g daily
- Plus fluoroquinolone OR macrolide

With Risk Factors for Pseudomonas (structural lung disease, recent antibiotics):
- Antipseudomonal beta-lactam: Cefepime 1-2 g Q8-12H OR Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g Q6H
- PLUS respiratory fluoroquinolone: Levofloxacin or Ciprofloxacin
- Consider aminoglycoside addition for severe cases

5.3 Severe CAP (ICU Admission)
Typical Regimen:
- Beta-lactam (cephalosporin preferred): Ceftriaxone 1-2 g Q12H
- PLUS macrolide: Azithromycin 500 mg daily
- PLUS fluoroquinolone: Levofloxacin 750 mg daily
OR
- Respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy if contraindications to above

With Risk Factors for MRSA (as above):
- Add Vancomycin: 15-20 mg/kg Q8-12H
- Plus cephalosporin and macrolide as above
- Plus fluoroquinolone for atypical coverage

With Risk Factors for Pseudomonas:
- Antipseudomonal beta-lactam: Cefepime or Piperacillin-tazobactam
- Plus aminoglycoside: Gentamicin or tobramycin
- Plus respiratory fluoroquinolone
- Monitor aminoglycoside levels (peak 8-10, trough 1-2)

5.4 Duration of Antibiotic Therapy
General Principles:
- Minimum 5 days for most patients
- Extend to 10-14 days if:
  * Severe disease (shock, mechanical ventilation)
  * Atypical pathogens (Legionella)
  * Complicated disease (empyema, abscess)
  * Immunocompromised

Clinical Stability Criteria for Discharge:
- Temperature ≤ 37.8°C for 48 hours off antipyretics
- Heart rate < 100
- Respiratory rate < 24
- Systolic BP ≥ 90
- Oral tolerance
- Improved mental status
- Oxygenation adequate on room air or home O2
- Chest imaging stability

5.5 De-escalation
- Narrow spectrum antibiotics after culture results
- Transition from IV to oral when clinically stable
- Consider step-down to beta-lactam alone or fluoroquinolone if coverage broadened initially
- Avoid unnecessary dual coverage

6. ADJUNCTIVE THERAPIES

6.1 Oxygen Support
- Goal: SpO2 > 91% (≥ 92% if hypercapnic risk)
- Nasal cannula: 1-3 L/min for mild hypoxemia
- Face mask: 5-8 L/min for moderate hypoxemia
- High-flow oxygen: 40-60% via face mask if severe
- Mechanical ventilation: Indicated for respiratory failure (RR > 35, severe distress)
- NIPPV (non-invasive): Consider for hypercapnic COPD exacerbation

6.2 Hydration
- IV fluids: 1-2 L normal saline initially
- Target: Euvolemia avoiding overload (pulmonary edema risk)
- Transition to PO fluids when tolerating
- Monitor urine output and renal function

6.3 Symptomatic Relief
- Analgesics for chest pain: Acetaminophen or NSAIDs (caution in dehydration)
- Cough suppression: Dextromethorphan (avoid in COPD)
- Bronchodilators: If obstructive airway disease component
- No routine steroids unless COPD exacerbation

6.4 Antivirals
- Influenza: Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) 75 mg BID × 5 days
  * Most effective if started within 48 hours
  * Consider regardless if severe illness or hospitalization
  * Reduces duration and severity of symptoms by ~1 day
- COVID-19: Depending on severity and local recommendations
  * Remdesivir for hospitalized with hypoxemia
  * Tocilizumab for severe with rapid deterioration

7. COMPLICATIONS

7.1 Pleural Complications
Parapneumonic Effusion:
- Uncomplicated: Exudative, sterile, resolves with antibiotics
- Complicated: Elevated glucose, positive Gram stain or culture
- Empyema: Pus in pleural space (frank positive culture)
- Drainage indicated if: positive culture, pH < 7.0, glucose < 50, LDH > 1000
- Ultrasound or CT: 50-60% sensitivity for complex effusion
- Thoracentesis: Diagnostic and therapeutic
- Chest tube or VATS: If loculated or not improving

7.2 Pneumothorax
- Risk: Primary spontaneous (young tall men), secondary (COPD)
- Management: Oxygen, chest tube if large/symptomatic
- Monitor for tension physiology (hypotension, neck vein distension)

7.3 Lung Abscess
- Usually follows aspiration pneumonia
- Symptoms: Foul-smelling sputum, fever
- Diagnosis: CXR or CT showing cavitation with air-fluid level
- Treatment: IV antibiotics covering anaerobes (clindamycin or beta-lactam-beta lactamase inhibitor)
- Drainage: If > 4 cm or not improving in 2-4 weeks

7.4 Sepsis and Septic Shock
- See separate sepsis guidelines
- Mortality significantly increased
- Early recognition and aggressive management critical

8. PREVENTION

8.1 Vaccinations
Pneumococcal Vaccination (PCV20 or PCV15 + PPSV23):
- All adults ≥ 19 years with chronic conditions
- All adults ≥ 65 years
- PCV15 given first, then PPSV23 at least 1 year later
- PCV20 is single vaccine alternative

Influenza Vaccination:
- Annual vaccination for all ≥ 6 months
- Reduced severity and mortality if infected
- Especially important for high-risk populations

COVID-19 Vaccination:
- Following current CDC recommendations
- Important for immunocompromised and elderly

8.2 Lifestyle Measures
- Smoking cessation
- Alcohol moderation
- Hand hygiene and respiratory etiquette
- Avoiding exposure to sick individuals

9. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

9.1 Elderly Patients
- Often present atypically (no fever, altered mental status)
- Higher mortality even with appropriate therapy
- More comorbidities requiring drug interactions assessment
- Consider more aggressive initial therapy

9.2 Immunocompromised
- HIV/AIDS: Include PCP prophylaxis if CD4 < 200
- Neutropenic: Rapid progression, consider Pseudomonas coverage
- Transplant recipients: Broad coverage plus consideration of fungal/viral pathogens
- Chemotherapy patients: High-risk populations

9.3 COPD and Asthma
- Higher mortality and complication rates
- Optimize bronchodilators and steroids
- Monitor for acute exacerbation component

9.4 Pregnancy
- Generally safe antibiotics: Beta-lactams, macrolides, fluoroquinolones
- Avoid tetracyclines
- Hospitalization if moderate-severe disease
