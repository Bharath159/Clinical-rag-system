COMPREHENSIVE HYPERTENSION MANAGEMENT GUIDELINES - 2023
====================================================================

1. CLASSIFICATION AND RISK STRATIFICATION

1.1 Blood Pressure Categories (ACC/AHA Guidelines)
Normal: SBP < 120 and DBP < 80 mmHg
Elevated: SBP 120-129 and DBP < 80 mmHg
Stage 1 Hypertension: SBP 130-139 or DBP 80-89 mmHg
Stage 2 Hypertension: SBP ≥ 140 or DBP ≥ 90 mmHg
Hypertensive Crisis: SBP > 180 and/or DBP > 120 mmHg

1.2 Hypertensive Urgency vs Emergency
Hypertensive Urgency: Elevated BP without acute end-organ damage
- Usually amenable to oral therapy
- Gradual reduction of BP often preferred to avoid stroke risk
- More common presentation (90% of hypertensive crises)

Hypertensive Emergency: Elevated BP with signs of acute end-organ damage
- Acute coronary syndrome
- Acute heart failure with pulmonary edema
- Hypertensive encephalopathy
- Intracranial hemorrhage
- Acute ischemic stroke
- Acute kidney injury or progressive renal disease
- Pre-eclampsia or eclampsia
- Aortic dissection
- Requires IV antihypertensive therapy

1.3 Risk Stratification
Cardiovascular Risk Assessment:
- 10-year cardiovascular risk using Framingham or ASCVD calculator
- Presence of hypertension-related target organ damage
- Chronic kidney disease staging (based on GFR and albuminuria)
- Diabetes mellitus or other comorbidities
- Family history of premature cardiovascular disease

2. DIAGNOSTIC EVALUATION

2.1 History Taking
Essential Information:
- Duration and severity of hypertension
- Severity and onset of current symptoms
- Orthostatic symptoms (dizziness, syncope)
- Headache characteristics: location, duration, severity
- Chest pain or dyspnea
- Vision changes or neurological symptoms
- Medication adherence and side effects
- Previous episodes of hypertensive crisis
- Recent cocaine or amphetamine use
- Medication compliance (including OTC decongestants)
- Dietary sodium and alcohol intake
- Episodes of claudication or rest pain

2.2 Physical Examination
Vital Signs:
- BP measurement: Both arms, seated and standing positions
- Orthostatic vital signs: Check for significant drop in BP on standing
- Heart rate: Document tachycardia or bradycardia
- Respiratory rate: Assess for respiratory distress
- Temperature: Rule out infection

Cardiovascular Examination:
- Cardiac auscultation: Listen for S3 gallop (heart failure), new murmurs
- Carotid pulses and bruits: Assess for arterial stenosis
- Peripheral pulses: Compare bilateral extremities for symmetry
- Abdominal examination: Check for aortic bruit or tenderness
- Lower extremity edema: Assess severity and distribution

Neurological Examination:
- Mental status: Assess for confusion, agitation, or altered consciousness
- Fundoscopic examination: Grade hypertensive retinopathy (papilledema, flame hemorrhages)
- Motor and sensory examination: Focal deficits suggesting stroke
- Deep tendon reflexes and gait: Assess for neuropathy

2.3 Diagnostic Testing
Laboratory Studies for Initial Evaluation:
- Basic metabolic panel: Electrolytes, glucose, creatinine, BUN for renal function
- Urinalysis: Proteinuria, hematuria indicating target organ damage
- Lipid panel: Complete lipid assessment including HDL, LDL, triglycerides
- Fasting glucose or HbA1c: Screen for diabetes
- Uric acid: Hyperuricemia associated with hypertension
- Thyroid function tests: TSH for secondary hypertension workup
- ECG: Baseline assessment for left ventricular hypertrophy, ischemia, arrhythmias
- Chest X-ray: Assess cardiac silhouette, pulmonary edema
- Renal ultrasound or CT: Consider for high-risk features suggesting secondary hypertension

Testing During Hypertensive Crisis:
- Troponin: Rule out acute coronary syndrome
- BNP/NT-proBNP: Assess for acute heart failure
- Blood glucose: Elevated glucose common in hypertensive emergency
- Magnesium and calcium levels: Affect vasoreactivity
- Repeat urinalysis: New hematuria or RBC casts suggest acute glomerulonephritis
- Blood gas analysis: Assess oxygenation and acid-base status if respiratory symptoms
- CT or MRI brain: Suspected stroke or intracranial hemorrhage

3. CHRONIC HYPERTENSION MANAGEMENT

3.1 Lifestyle Modifications (Part of all treatment)
Dietary Approaches:
- DASH diet: Rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins
- Sodium reduction: Target < 2300 mg/day (ideal 1500 mg/day)
- Potassium-rich foods: Bananas, spinach, sweet potatoes, beans
- Reduce alcohol: Men 2 drinks/day, women 1 drink/day
- Limit caffeine: Acute effect on BP, consider individual response

Physical Activity:
- Aerobic exercise: 150 minutes moderate intensity per week
- Resistance training: 2 days/week of muscle strengthening
- Activities: Brisk walking, jogging, cycling, swimming
- Start gradually: Important for sedentary individuals

Weight Management:
- Target BMI 18.5-24.9 kg/m²
- Weight loss of 5-10% improves BP in overweight/obese patients
- Reduces cardiovascular risk beyond BP benefits

Stress Reduction and Sleep:
- Meditation, yoga, or tai chi: 30 minutes daily
- Sleep 7-9 hours daily: Sleep apnea screening with Epworth scale
- Smoking cessation: Critical for cardiovascular risk reduction

3.2 Pharmacological Treatment Priorities
Initial Drug Selection Based on Compelling Indications:

ACE Inhibitors or ARBs (First-line):
- Indications: Diabetes, CKD, post-MI, heart failure, albuminuria
- Examples: Lisinopril 10 mg daily, Losartan 50 mg daily
- Benefit: Renal protection, LV remodeling prevention
- Monitor: Creatinine, potassium for hyperkalemia

Thiazide/Thiazide-like Diuretics (First-line):
- Indications: Metabolic syndrome, fluid overload, isolated systolic hypertension
- Examples: HCTZ 25-50 mg daily, Chlorthalidone 12.5-25 mg daily
- Benefit: Cardiovascular risk reduction, potent in older adults
- Monitor: Glucose, lipids, uric acid, hypokalemia

Calcium Channel Blockers (First-line):
- Indications: Coronary artery disease, paroxysmal SVT, advanced age
- Examples: Amlodipine 5-10 mg daily, Diltiazem 180-240 mg daily
- Benefit: No metabolic effects, can be combined with other agents
- Monitor: Reflex tachycardia with dihydropyridines, constipation

Beta-Blockers (Specific Indications):
- Indications: Post-MI, heart failure with reduced EF, coronary artery disease, tachyarrhythmias
- Examples: Metoprolol 50-100 mg BID, Carvedilol 12.5-25 mg daily
- Less effective as monotherapy for uncomplicated hypertension
- Monitor: Bradycardia, bronchospasm, fatigue

Alpha-Blockers (Specific Indications):
- Indications: Symptomatic benign prostatic hyperplasia
- Examples: Doxazosin 4-8 mg daily
- Not first-line for hypertension alone due to inferior cardiovascular outcomes
- Monitor: Orthostatic hypotension, syncope

3.3 Target Blood Pressure Goals
General Adult Population:
- Goal BP < 130/80 mmHg (based on SPRINT trial evidence)
- More intensive control reduces CV events but requires monitoring for adverse effects

Special Populations:
Diabetes Mellitus:
- Goal BP < 130/80 mmHg
- SGLT2 inhibitors recommended if CKD present
- ACE-I or ARB preferred

Chronic Kidney Disease:
- Goal BP < 130/80 mmHg (more intensive if proteinuria present)
- ACE-I or ARB first-line to slow renal disease progression
- Avoid NSAIDs

Aged ≥ 65 years:
- Goal BP < 130/80 mmHg but individualize based on tolerance and frailty
- Slow titration to avoid orthostatic symptoms
- Regular fall risk assessment

4. ACUTE HYPERTENSIVE EMERGENCY MANAGEMENT

4.1 General Approach
Time to Initiate Therapy: Immediately (within minutes)
Blood Pressure Reduction Target:
- First 1 hour: Reduce MAP by 10-20% (or SBP by 15-25 mmHg)
- Next 2-6 hours: Continued careful reduction to 160/100-110 mmHg
- Avoid excessive rapid reduction: Risk of stroke or MI from decreased cerebral/coronary perfusion

Monitoring:
- Continuous cardiac monitoring and pulse oximetry
- Continuous or frequent NIBP monitoring every 5-15 minutes initially
- Ideally use arterial line for continuous BP monitoring
- Frequent neurological checks
- Urine output monitoring (foley catheter)
- Serial labs: Electrolytes, creatinine, lactate as needed

4.2 Intravenous Antihypertensive Agents

Nitroprusside (Rapid onset, very potent):
- Initial dose: 0.3-1 mcg/kg/min IV infusion
- Titration: Increase by 0.5-1 mcg/kg/min every 5-10 minutes
- Maximum dose: 2-3 mcg/kg/min (higher risk of toxicity)
- Duration: 1-2 minutes after cessation
- Utility: Most potent IV agent, useful for severe hypertensive emergency
- Cautions: Cyanide toxicity with prolonged use; monitor thiocyanate levels after 72 hours
- Requires ICU monitoring and arterial line

Nitroglycerin (Mild-moderate reduction):
- Sublingual: 0.3-0.6 mg every 5-15 minutes
- IV infusion: 5-100 mcg/min, titrate every 3-5 minutes
- Duration: 5-10 minutes
- Utility: Preferred for hypertension with acute coronary syndrome or heart failure
- Advantage: Improves coronary perfusion
- Disadvantage: Tolerance develops with prolonged use

Labetalol (Balanced effects, moderate onset):
- IV bolus: 20 mg over 2 minutes, then 40-80 mg every 10 minutes (max 220 mg)
- IV infusion: 2 mg/min, titrate to effect (max 160 mg/min)
- Duration: 3-6 hours
- Utility: Excellent choice for most hypertensive emergencies including pregnancy
- Advantage: Combined alpha and beta blockade
- Caution: Avoid in bradycardia, asthma, or heart block

Nicardipine (Moderate onset, good titrability):
- IV infusion: 5 mg/hr initially, increase by 2.5 mg/hr every 5-15 minutes
- Usual range: 15-30 mg/hr
- Duration: 15-30 minutes after cessation
- Utility: Excellent for most hypertensive emergencies
- Advantage: Very predictable BP lowering, can transition to oral quickly
- Disadvantage: Peripheral infusion acceptable but central line preferred

Hydralazine (Slower onset, less predictable):
- IV dose: 10-20 mg every 4-6 hours
- IM dose: 10-50 mg every 4-6 hours
- Onset: 10-30 minutes IV; 20-30 minutes IM
- Duration: 3-8 hours
- Utility: Useful in pregnancy-related hypertensive emergencies
- Disadvantage: Unpredictable response, reflex tachycardia, lupus-like syndrome with chronic use

Phentolamine (Alpha-blockade for catecholamine excess):
- IV bolus: 5-15 mg every 5-10 minutes
- Infusion: 0.5-1.4 mg/min
- Onset: Immediate
- Duration: Very short (15-30 minutes)
- Utility: Pheochromocytoma crisis, cocaine-induced hypertensive emergency
- Important: Use beta-blockers AFTER alpha-blockade to avoid reflex tachycardia

Esmolol (Rapid onset/offset, beta-blockade):
- Loading: 0.5-1 mg/kg IV over 1 minute
- Infusion: 50-200 mcg/kg/min
- Onset: < 5 minutes
- Duration: < 10 minutes after cessation
- Utility: Intraoperative and postoperative hypertension, aortic dissection
- Advantage: Very short acting, easily titrated
- Caution: Use with vasodilators for dissection

4.3 Specific Clinical Scenarios

Hypertensive Emergency with Acute MI:
- Treat with nitroglycerin + beta-blocker combination
- Avoid positive inotropes; use nitrates for preload reduction
- Target: Reduce SBP 15-25% in first 1 hour
- ACE inhibitor once stabilized

Hypertensive Emergency with Acute Stroke:
- CRITICAL: Avoid aggressive BP lowering in acute ischemic stroke
- Only lower BP if SBP > 220 mmHg or DBP > 120 mmHg before thrombolytics
- For hemorrhagic stroke: Target SBP < 140 mmHg
- Use labetalol or nicardipine for controlled reduction

Hypertensive Emergency with Pulmonary Edema:
- Nitroglycerin IV: Reduces preload and improves oxygenation
- IV diuretics: Furosemide 20-40 mg IV for fluid overload
- Nicardipine or labetalol for additional BP control
- Consider noninvasive ventilation (CPAP/BiPAP)

Pre-eclampsia and Eclampsia:
- Avoid ACE inhibitors and ARBs in pregnancy
- Labetalol: First-line for acute severe hypertension
- Hydralazine: Alternative agent
- Magnesium sulfate seizure prophylaxis
- Obstetric consultation mandatory

Aortic Dissection:
- Immediate ICU admission
- Target: SBP < 120 mmHg within 20 minutes
- Use combination: Esmolol or labetalol PLUS nitroprusside or nicardipine
- Beta-blockade FIRST to reduce dP/dT (rate of change of pressure)
- Emergent CT or TEE imaging
- Surgical consultation

Pheochromocytoma Crisis:
- Alpha-blockade FIRST with phentolamine or nifedipine
- Then beta-blockade with esmolol after alpha-blockade established
- NEVER use beta-blockers alone (unopposed alpha)
- IV fluids to expand intravascular volume
- Surgical exploration

5. TRANSITION TO CHRONIC THERAPY

5.1 Timing of Transition
- Once hypertensive emergency controlled with IV agents
- BP stabilized at target range
- Patient able to tolerate oral medications
- Usually 24-72 hours of IV therapy

5.2 Agent Selection for Transition
- Transition to agents already on board (home medications) if tolerated
- Add agents for specific indications if not already present
- ACE-I/ARB if diabetic or renal disease
- Beta-blocker if CAD or heart failure
- Thiazide diuretic if volume overload or metabolic syndrome
- Calcium channel blocker as alternative to other agents

5.3 Discharge Planning
- Continue antihypertensive regimen from transition
- Titrate per outpatient BP readings
- Follow-up within 1 week
- Patient education on medication adherence, lifestyle modification
- Address modifiable risk factors
- Consider referral to hypertension specialist if secondary cause identified

6. SECONDARY HYPERTENSION WORKUP

Indications for Workup:
- Hypertension onset before age 30 or after age 60
- Resistant hypertension (uncontrolled on 3 drugs)
- Hypertensive emergency without prior HTN
- Sudden change in BP pattern
- Associated symptoms

Common Secondary Causes:
Renal artery stenosis: Severe HTN, refractory to therapy, risk for flash pulmonary edema
Pheochromocytoma: Severe episodic HTN with sweating, tremor, anxiety, palpitations
Primary hyperaldosteronism: Hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis
Cushings syndrome: Hypertension with Central obesity, striae, proximal weakness
Hyperthyroidism: Elevated HR, palpitations, heat intolerance
Sleep apnea: Daytime somnolence, snoring, morning headaches
Coarctation of aorta: Young age, upper extremity HTN > lower extremity, cardiac murmur
