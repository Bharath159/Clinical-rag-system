1. PEDIATRIC BASIC LIFE SUPPORT (BLS)

1.1 Principles of Pediatric CPR

Key Differences from Adults:
- Cardiac arrest in children usually secondary (respiratory cause most common)
- Prevention and early intervention more important
- Smaller chest: Different compression technique
- Higher metabolic rate
- Faster deterioration without oxygen
- Better neurological outcomes possible with aggressive interventions

Age Categories for Pediatric ACLS (2020 AHA Standards):
- Infant: < 1 year
- Child: 1-12 years
- Adolescent: 12-14 years (can use adult pads if unavailable)
- Transition at puberty (chest development)

1.2 Recognition and Response

Assessment of Consciousness:
- Tap shoulders and shout: "Are you okay?"
- Check for responsiveness and signs of life
- Do NOT assume unresponsiveness = cardiac arrest
- Look for gasping (NOT a sign of life; indicates need for CPR)

Initial Response (First 30 seconds):
- Verify scene safety
- Activate Emergency Medical Services (call 911 or equivalent)
- Retrieve AED if available
- Place child on firm, flat surface
- Remove restrictive clothing
- Prepare for CPR

1.3 Compression Technique

Infants (< 1 year):
- Compression site: Lower third of sternum (below nipple line)
- Method: Two-fingers (index and middle of one hand) or two thumb encircling technique
- Hand position: Thumbs on lower sternum, fingers encircling chest
- Depth: At least 1/3 of chest depth (approximately 1.5 inches; 4 cm)
- Rate: 100-120 compressions/minute
- Newborns may not need CPR if cord still attached (cut cord first)

Children (1-12 years):
- Compression site: Lower half of breastbone (below nipple line)
- Method: One hand (if small) or heel of two hands (if larger child)
- Hand position: On lower half of sternum, two fingers above xiphoid
- Depth: At least 1/3 of chest depth (approximately 2 inches; 5 cm)
- Rate: 100-120 compressions/minute
- Push hard and push fast (maintain rate, don't exceed)

Adolescents (> 12 years):
- Same as adult CPR
- Compression site: Center of chest (between nipples)
- Depth: At least 2 inches (5 cm) in adults/large adolescents
- Rate: 100-120 compressions/minute
- Use heel of one or two hands depending on size

1.4 Ventilation

Resue Breaths for Unresponsive Non-Breathing Children:
- Essential for children (better outcomes than compression alone in pediatric arrest)
- Rate: 1 breath after every 30 compressions (30:2 ratio)
- Depth: Moderate (watch for chest rise without overinflation)
- Duration: 1 second per breath
- Avoid gastric distention (too much volume, too fast)

Rescue Breath Technique:
- Head-tilt chin-lift (open airway)
- Pinch nose closed if mouth-to-mouth
- Sealed pocket mask, if available
- BVM preferred if trained (avoids mouth-to-mouth)
- Bag size: Appropriately sized for child (not adult bag)
- Rate: 12-20 breaths/minute if intubated (1 every 3-5 seconds)

Airway Opening:
- Head-tilt: Lifting forehead back
- Chin-lift: Lifting angle of mandible upward
- Caution: Neck hyperextension unnecessary and potentially harmful
- Avoid aggressive airway maneuvers if spinal injury suspected

1.5 Defibrillation with AED

When to Use AED:
- As soon as available for any unresponsive child without pulse
- Witnessed sudden collapse (higher probability of shockable rhythm)
- Do NOT delay compressions to search for AED

Ped-Paddles or Dose Attenuator:
- Infants < 1 year: Defibrillation pad optional (limited evidence, may consider)
- Children 1-8 years or < 25 kg: Pediatric pads or dose attenuator (reduces energy)
- > 8 years or > 25 kg: Adult pads and adult doses acceptable

AED Operation:
- Power on
- Attach pads per manufacturer instructions
- Ensure no one touching child
- Analyze rhythm
- If shock indicated: Ensure clear, deliver shock
- Resume CPR immediately after (don't check pulse)
- Recheck rhythm every 2 minutes (every other check of pulse)

2. PEDIATRIC SEPSIS RECOGNITION

2.1 Definition and Epidemiology

Pediatric Sepsis Definition:
- Systemic inflammatory response to suspected or confirmed infection
- With at least two of four SIRS criteria:
  * Fever (> 38.5°C) or hypothermia (< 36°C)
  * Tachycardia (> 2 SD above age mean)
  * Tachypnea (> 2 SD above age-appropriate rate)
  * Leukocytosis (> 12,000), leukopenia (< 4,000), or > 10% bands or immature forms

Epidemiology:
- Incidence: 0.5-6.4 cases per 1,000 PICU admissions
- Most common in hospitalized children
- Mortality: 2-10% in developed countries (higher in resource-limited settings)
- Risk factors: Prematurity, immunodeficiency, indwelling catheters, recent surgery

2.2 Clinical Recognition of Sepsis and Shock

Early Warning Signs of Sepsis:
- Fever with petechial or purpuric rash
- Fever with extremity pain (indicates possible meningococcemia)
- Fever with altered mental status or irritability
- Fever with respiratory distress disproportionate to illness
- Fever with poor perfusion signs
- Fever in very young (< 3 months) requires aggressive evaluation

Pediatric Septic Shock Clinical Presentation:
- Often presents as "cold shock" (vasoconstriction):
  * Poor perfusion (mottled skin, prolonged capillary refill > 2-3 seconds)
  * Tachycardia
  * Cool extremities (core to peripheral temperature gradient)
  * Oliguria
  * Acidosis
- Or "warm shock" (vasodilation):
  * Bounding pulses
  * Normal to elevated cardiac output
  * Warm extremities
  * Wide pulse pressure
  * Low systemic vascular resistance
  * Often progresses to cold shock if untreated

2.3 Age-Specific Vital Sign Parameters

Normal Heart Rates (at rest):
- Infants (< 1 year): 100-160 bpm
- Toddlers (1-3 years): 90-150 bpm
- Preschool (3-6 years): 80-120 bpm
- School age (6-12 years): 70-110 bpm
- Adolescents (> 12 years): 60-100 bpm

Tachycardia thresholds (for sepsis definition):
- > 160 bpm in infants
- > 150 bpm in toddlers
- > 130 bpm in preschool
- > 110 bpm in school age
- > 100 bpm in adolescents

Normal Respiratory Rates:
- Infants (< 1 year): 30-60 breaths/min
- Toddlers (1-3 years): 24-40 breaths/min
- Preschool (3-6 years): 22-34 breaths/min
- School age (6-12 years): 18-30 breaths/min
- Adolescents (> 12 years): 12-16 breaths/min

Tachypnea thresholds (for sepsis definition):
- > 60 breaths/min in infants
- > 50 breaths/min in toddlers
- > 40 breaths/min in preschool
- > 30 breaths/min in school age
- > 25 breaths/min in adolescents

Blood Pressure (varies widely by age, weight, gender):
- Rough estimate: Systolic BP = 90 + (2 × age in years) ± 20
- Hypotension in septic shock: < 5th percentile for age
- Refer to pediatric BP charts for exact values

2.4 Sepsis Scoring

pSOFA (Pediatric Quick qSOFA):
Simplified assessment:
- Respiratory rate abnormal for age
- Altered mentation (GCS < 15 or decrease of 3)
- Systolic BP < 5th percentile for age
Score ≥ 2: High-risk for poor outcome

Organs scoring system (for sepsis definition):
- Cardiovascular: Hypotension for age, OR need for vasopressor, OR lactate > 2 mmol/L
- Respiratory: PaO2/FiO2 < 300, OR PaCO2 > 50, OR need for > 40% oxygen, OR intubation
- Hematologic: Platelets < 100,000, OR INR > 1.5, OR aPTT > 60
- Hepatic: Total bilirubin > 1.5 × normal, OR ALT/AST > 3 × normal
- Neurological: GCS < 11
- Renal: Creatinine > 1.8 × age normal, OR oliguria

3. PEDIATRIC SEPSIS MANAGEMENT

3.1 Initial Assessment and Stabilization

First 15 Minutes (Golden Hour - Actually First Hour):
A, B, C Approach:
A (Airway):
- Assess airway patency
- Position for airway opening if needed
- Prepare equipment for airway support
- Do NOT delay IV access or fluids for airway

B (Breathing):
- Assess work of breathing
- Apply oxygen to achieve SpO2 > 94%
- Assist ventilation if RR too slow, SpO2 < 90%, respiratory muscle fatigue
- Prepare for intubation if resp distress severe

C (Circulation):
- Establish IV or IO (intraosseous) access
- Assess perfusion (cap refill, skin mottling, extremity temperature, pulses)
- Begin fluid resuscitation
- Obtain blood for labs

Immediately:
- Expose child to assess for petechiae/purpura (meningococcemia hallmark)
- Continue BLS if needed
- Summon experienced help/PICU team
- Prepare for rapid sequence intubation if needed

3.2 Vascular Access

Order of Preference (Seconds to Attempt Each):
1. Peripheral IV: Largest gauge feasible (18 G if possible, up to 5 seconds)
2. Intraosseous (IO): If < 30 seconds on peripherals unsuccessful
   - Most reliable in pediatric emergency, especially < 6 years
   - Single anterior tibial approach below tibial tubercle
   - Provides same access as central line for fluids/meds
   - Complications rare if removed within 24 hours
3. Central line: After stabilization if needed for ongoing support

Intravenous Fluid Calculation:
- Frequent medication administration
- Fluid resuscitation
- Allow large-bore IV (20G minimum for fluid boluses)
- Multiple attempts on same extremity not recommended; switch extremities

3.3 Initial Laboratory Tests

Immediate Labs to Obtain:
- Complete blood count (CBC): Infection pattern, platelet count
- Comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP): Electrolytes, kidney function, glucose
- Lactate: Perfusion marker, prognostic indicator
- Blood gas (arterial or venous): pH, acidosis assessment
- Procalcitonin or CRP: Inflammatory markers (if available, aids diagnosis)
- Liver function tests: Assess organ dysfunction
- Coagulation studies (PT/INR, aPTT): Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) screen
- Blood cultures: Always before antibiotics (hold results for organism identification)
- Urinalysis and urine culture: If UTI suspected
- Site-specific cultures: Based on source

3.4 Fluid Resuscitation

Initial Resuscitation Goal:
- Restore perfusion
- Improve lactate
- Target: Capillary refill < 2-3 seconds, visible urine output, normal mental status

Fluid Selection:
- Crystalloid: 20 mL/kg bolus IV/IO over 15 minutes
- Choice: Normal saline (NS) or lactated Ringer's (LR)
- Can repeat bolus once if signs of shock persist
- Maximum initial: 60 mL/kg (three boluses)
- Hypotonic solutions (< 0.9%) avoided
- Colloids (albumin, FFP): Consider after initial crystalloid if massive volumes needed

Calculation Example:
- 20 kg child: 20 × 20 = 400 mL IV bolus over 15 minutes
- Repeat if still in shock
- Total of 3 boluses maximum (1200 mL) before reassessing

Fluid Bolus Administration:
- Direct IV line preferred (faster than through pump)
- Rapid infusion initially over 15 minutes
- Reassess perfusion after each bolus
- Monitor for fluid overload (hepatomegaly, crackles, increased respiratory effort)
- Continue maintenance + deficit + ongoing losses

Maintenance Fluid Calculation (Holliday-Segar):
- First 10 kg: 100 mL/kg/day
- Second 10 kg: 50 mL/kg/day
- Each kg > 20: 20 mL/kg/day
Example: 25 kg child = (10 × 100) + (10 × 50) + (5 × 20) = 1000 + 500 + 100 = 1600 mL/day

3.5 Vasopressor Support

Indications:
- Persistent hypotension after two 20 mL/kg boluses of crystalloid
- Persistent signs of shock (cap refill > 2 seconds, oliguria, lactate > 4)
- Clinical deterioration despite maximum fluid therapy

Agent Selection:

Epinephrine (Adrenaline, First-line for Cold Shock):
- Initial dose: 0.01 mg/kg IV (use 1:10,000 concentration)
- Infusion: 0.1-5 mcg/kg/min titrate to effect (start 0.1 mcg/kg/min)
- Effects: Alpha and beta (dose-dependent; beta at lower doses, alpha at higher)
- Titration: Increase by 0.1-0.2 mcg/kg/min every 5-10 minutes
- Monitor: HR, BP, perfusion, urine output, lactate
- Goal: Restore systolic BP > 5th percentile, cap refill < 2 seconds, urine output > 1 mL/kg/hour

Normetanephrine/Norepinephrine:
- Initial infusion: 0.05-1.0 mcg/kg/min
- Titrate to effect (up to 2.0+ mcg/kg/min)
- Primary alpha effect with some beta (less than epi)
- Use: Warm shock with low systemic resistance
- May be used as first-line in some protocols (varies by center)

Dobutamine:
- Dose: 2-10 mcg/kg/min IV infusion
- Inotropic agent: Increases contractility
- Some vasodilation: May drop BP if used without other agents
- Use: Combination with epinephrine for low cardiac output state
- Caution: Tachycardia side effect important in children

Vasopressin:
- May be added to epinephrine if inadequate response
- Dose: 0.0003-0.002 units/kg/min
- Fixed low-dose or higher dose strategies
- Limited pediatric evidence vs adults

Vasopressor Infusion Preparation:
- Epinephrine: 0.6 × weight (kg) in mL of 1:10,000 solution in 50 mL bag = 0.1 mcg/kg/min at 1 mL/hr
- Higher concentrations if rapid escalation of rates needed
- Preferably via central line (peripheral acceptable briefly)

3.6 Antibiotic Administration

Timing:
- CRITICAL: Within 1 hour of recognition (same as adults)
- Do NOT delay for procedures or imaging
- Cultures obtained before antibiotics when feasible
- If cultures not obtained: Start antibiotics immediately (blood loss worse than missing culture)

Empiric Antibiotic Selection (Based on Source):

Community-Acquired (CAP presenting as sepsis):
- Ceftriaxone 50-80 mg/kg/day (divided TID-QID) + Azithromycin 10 mg/kg day 1, then 5 mg/kg daily
- For MRSA risk: Add Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg Q6-8H
- For Pseudomonas risk: Use broader spectrum beta-lactam

Haemophilus influenzae Risk (Unvaccinated):
- Ceftriaxone or Cefotaxime (crosses blood-brain barrier well)

Meningitis (Suspected CNS source):
- Ceftriaxone 80 mg/kg/day (TID-QID) + Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg Q6H
- Add Ampicillin if < 3 months (Listeria coverage)
- Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg Q6H × 4 days (meningitis-associated complications reduction)

Gram-Negative (Sepsis with abdominal or urinary source):
- Piperacillin-tazobactam (90 mg/kg/day of piperacillin divided Q6-8H)
- OR Cephalosporin + Metronidazole (anaerobe coverage)
- Consider aminoglycoside for Pseudomonas coverage

3.7 Source Control

Surgical Drainage:
- Abdominal abscess: Percutaneous or surgical drainage critical
- Delayed source control associated with higher mortality
- Necrotizing soft tissue: Requires immediate surgical debridement

Central Line Removal:
- If line-related sepsis suspected: Remove catheter
- Send segment for culture
- Replace if continues need

Other Source Control:
- Foley catheter if infected: Remove and consider new placement
- Wound management for infected surgical wounds
- Appendicitis: Appendectomy

3.8 Monitoring and Ongoing Support

Continuous Monitoring:
- Cardiac rhythm
- Pulse oximetry
- Frequent vital signs
- Neurological status
- Urine output (target 1 mL/kg/hour)
- Reassess perfusion frequently (goal cap refill < 2 seconds, skin color normalization)

Lab Monitoring:
- Serial lactate (most important; goal < 2 mmol/L)
- Repeat CBC, CMP, coagulation studies every 6-24 hours depending on severity
- Blood cultures for sterilization if possible

Respiratory Support:
- Oxygen supplementation to SpO2 > 94%
- Intubation if:
  * GCS < 12 (airway protection)
  * Severe respiratory distress
  * Inability to maintain SpO2
  * Planned procedures requiring sedation

Ratios for Intubation:
- Induction agent: Ketamine preferred (maintains BP), etomidate alternative
- Paralytic: Rocuronium or vecuronium (depolarizing agents less preferred in acute setting)
- Pre-oxygenation critical (pediatric desaturation rapid)

4. PEDIATRIC SHOCK CLASSIFICATION

4.1 Warm Shock vs Cold Shock

Cold Shock (Distributive → Hypovolemic):
- Pale, mottled skin
- Cool extremities
- Delayed cap refill (> 2 seconds)
- Weak pulses
- Tachycardia
- Hypotension (late finding)
- Low urine output
- Altered mentation
- Requires fluid + vasopressors

Warm Shock (Distributive → Early):
- Flushed skin
- Bounding pulses
- Brisk cap refill (< 1 second)
- Warm extremities
- Wide pulse pressure
- Normal or elevated BP early
- Possible high urine output initially
- May progress to cold shock without intervention
- Requires fluids primarily; vasopressors if deterioration

5. POST-RESUSCITATION CARE

5.1 PICU Admission
- All septic children requiring vasopressors
- Altered mental status
- Significant organ dysfunction
- Requirement for advanced ventilation

5.2 Antibiotic De-escalation
- After culture results and organism identification
- Narrow to narrowest effective spectrum
- Typically after 48-72 hours of therapy

5.3 Duration of Therapy
- Sepsis source-dependent
- Typically 10-14 days minimum
- Longer for complicated sources (meningitis, endocarditis)

5.4 Transition Planning
- Discharge when:
  * Hemodynamically stable off vasopressors
  * Tolerating oral/feeding
  * Mental status normalized
  * Labs improving
  * Can complete antibiotics as outpatient
- Infectious disease follow-up if complicated course
- Reassess vaccination status (post-illness)
