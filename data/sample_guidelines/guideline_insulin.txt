INSULIN MANAGEMENT AND DIABETIC KETOACIDOSIS GUIDELINES - 2023
====================================================================

1. PHYSIOLOGICAL OVERVIEW

1.1 Insulin Secretion and Metabolism
Normal Physiology:
- Insulin synthesized in pancreatic beta cells (islets of Langerhans)
- Released in two phases: Immediate phase (2-5 minutes) and sustained phase (gradual release)
- Half-life: 5-6 minutes in circulation; metabolized by kidneys and liver
- Acts via tyrosine kinase receptors on cell surfaces
- Glucose-dependent secretion; stimulated by elevated blood glucose

Physiology of Insulin Action:
- Glucose uptake: Enhanced in muscle and adipose tissue via GLUT4 translocation
- Protein synthesis: Amino acid uptake and incorporation into proteins
- Lipogenesis: Inhibits lipolysis; promotes fat storage
- Antiketotic effect: Suppresses fatty acid oxidation and ketone production
- Inhibits hepatic glucose output: Reduces gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis

1.2 Glucose Counterregulation
Hormones Opposing Insulin:
- Glucagon: Released when glucose < 70 mg/dL; increases hepatic glucose output
- Catecholamines (epinephrine/norepinephrine): Increase glucose, decrease insulin secretion
- Cortisol: Increases gluconeogenesis and peripheral insulin resistance
- Growth hormone: Impairs glucose utilization, increases lipolysis

Compromise in Type 1 Diabetes:
- Absolute insulin deficiency
- Beta cell destruction (autoimmune)
- Loss of glucagon response after ~5 years disease duration
- Increased susceptibility to ketoacidosis

2. INSULIN PREPARATIONS AND PHARMACOKINETICS

2.1 Bolus (Rapid-Acting) Insulins

Humalog (Insulin Lispro):
- Onset: 5-15 minutes
- Peak: 60-90 minutes
- Duration: 4-6 hours
- Concentration: 100 U/mL standard
- Formulation: U-100 vials, pens, or pumps
- Advantages: Rapid onset, predictable peak
- Used: With meals or correction doses

NovoLog (Insulin Aspart):
- Onset: 10-20 minutes
- Peak: 40-50 minutes
- Duration: 3-5 hours
- Similar efficacy and profile to lispro
- Interchangeable with lispro in most situations
- Available in vials, pens, and pump compatibility

Apidra (Insulin Glulisine):
- Onset: 10-20 minutes
- Peak: 50-60 minutes
- Duration: 4-6 hours
- Least used clinically but comparable profile

Regular Insulin (Crystalline Zinc Insulin):
- Onset: 30-60 minutes
- Peak: 2-3 hours
- Duration: 5-8 hours
- Advantages: Longest track record, inexpensive, IV stable
- Disadvantages: Slower onset, longer duration than analogs
- Used: When rapid-acting not available or for IV infusions

2.2 Intermediate-Acting Insulins

NPH Insulin (Neutral Protamine Hagedorn):
- Onset: 2-4 hours
- Peak: 6-10 hours (variable, sometimes biphasic)
- Duration: 10-16 hours (highly variable between individuals)
- Concentration: 100 U/mL
- Formulation: Cloudy suspension; requires mixing
- Advantages: Inexpensive, long acting
- Disadvantages: Variable action, injection twice daily often needed, hypoglycemia risk
- Used: Basal insulin or mixed formulations

2.3 Long-Acting (Basal) Insulins

Lantus (Insulin Glargine U-100):
- Onset: 2-4 hours
- Peak: Peakless (relatively flat profile)
- Duration: > 24 hours (up to 36 hours)
- Concentration: 100 U/mL or U-300
- Advantages: Predictable, once-daily dosing, true basal insulin
- Disadvantages: Cannot mix in syringe with other insulins (unless using pens for separate injection)
- Used: Daily basal insulin, once daily usually sufficient

Levemir (Insulin Detemir):
- Onset: 2-4 hours
- Peak: Some peak effect at 6-8 hours (less pronounced than NPH)
- Duration: 15-24 hours
- Concentration: 100 U/mL
- Advantages: Once or twice daily options, Flexible dosing
- Disadvantages: Usually requires twice-daily dosing for full 24-hour coverage
- Used: Basal insulin, flexibility in dosing

Tresiba (Insulin Degludec):
- Onset: 6-8 hours
- Peak: Peakless (ultra-long acting)
- Duration: > 42 hours
- Concentration: 100 U/mL or U-200
- Advantages: Very long acting, flexible dosing days, less hypoglycemia risk
- Disadvantages: Highest cost, delayed onset
- Used: Once-daily basal insulin with good glycemic control

2.4 Mixed Insulin Preparations

70/30 Insulin (NPH/Regular):
- 70% NPH, 30% Regular
- Onset: 30-60 minutes (regular component)
- Peak: 2-4 hours (regular), 6-10 hours (NPH)
- Duration: 10-16 hours
- Given twice daily with meals
- Less flexible than basal-bolus regimens

50/50 Insulin (NPH/Regular):
- Similar characteristics to 70/30
- Less frequently used
- Greater rapid-acting component

LysproMix 75/25 (NPL/Lispro):
- 75% protamine lispro, 25% lispro
- Faster onset than 70/30 due to lispro component
- Given before meals

Humulin/Novolin (Various Ratios Available):
- Similar principles with different ratios
- Pre-mixed convenience but less flexible dosing

3. INSULIN INITIATION IN TYPE 2 DIABETES

3.1 Indications for Insulin Therapy
Type 2 diabetes initiation considerations:
- Failing on other agents (metformin, GLP-1 RA, SGLT2-I, DPP-4-i, sulfonylurea)
- HbA1c repeatedly > 8-9% despite maximal oral therapy
- Presenting with new diabetes and significant hyperglycemia (glucose > 250-300 mg/dL)
- Pregnancy with gestational diabetes not controlled on other agents
- Renal insufficiency (eGFR < 30) limiting oral drug options
- Acute severe hyperglycemia (> 400 mg/dL) during acute illness
- DKA or HHS (requires insulin)

3.2 Basal Insulin Initiation (Most Common Approach)

Starting Dose:
- Weight-based: 0.1-0.2 units/kg daily
- Example: 100 kg patient = 10-20 units daily
- Conservative approach: Start 10 units daily or 0.1 units/kg
- Adjust upward more aggressively (2-4 units every 3-7 days) if needed

Titration Strategy:
- Review fasting glucose every 3-7 days
- Target fasting glucose: 90-130 mg/dL
- Increase basal insulin by 2-4 units (or 10% dose) if fasting glucose > 130
- Check for daytime/evening hyperglycemia; may need bolus insulin addition
- Once fasting glucose controlled, reassess HbA1c at 2-3 months

Addition of Bolus Insulin:
- If fasting glucose controlled but daytime/postmeal hyperglycemia persists
- Add rapid-acting insulin 4-10 units with largest meal initially
- Titrate based on pre-meal and 2-hour post-meal glucose patterns
- Full basal-bolus regimen: Basal insulin daily + rapid-acting with each meal

3.3 Basal-Bolus Regimen (Intensive Insulin Therapy)

Regimen Components:
Basal (Background) Insulin:
- Long-acting insulin (Lantus, Levemir, Tresiba)
- Administered once daily (or split into twice daily for Levemir)
- Goal: Maintain fasting glucose < 130 mg/dL, suppress hepatic glucose output
- Typically 40-50% of total daily insulin needs

Bolus (Mealtime) Insulin:
- Rapid-acting insulin (Humalog, Novolag, Apidra)
- Administered before main meals (breakfast, lunch, dinner)
- Doses calculated based on:
  * Carbohydrate content of meal (carb counting/carb ratios)
  * Current blood glucose (correction factor)
  * Target glucose
  * Individual sensitivity
- Typically 50-60% of total daily insulin needs

3.4 Insulin Dosing Calculations

Carbohydrate-to-Insulin Ratio (CIR):
- Determines mealtime bolus dose
- Calculated as: 1 unit of insulin per X grams of carbohydrates
- Example: 1:10 ratio = 1 unit per 10g carbs
- A 60g meal with 1:10 ratio = 6 units bolus
- Varies individually; often 1:10 to 1:20 in type 2 diabetes

Correction Factor (Insulin Sensitivity Factor):
- Determines how much 1 unit of insulin lowers glucose
- Calculated as: 1 unit of insulin lowers glucose by X mg/dL
- Example: 1:50 factor = 1 unit lowers glucose 50 mg/dL
- Varies individually; often 1:40 to 1:60 in type 2 diabetes
- Estimated using: 1800/total daily insulin units (or 1500 for more conservative)

Mealtime Insulin Calculation:
Bolus dose = (Carbs/CIR) + ((Current Glucose - Target) / Correction Factor)

Example Calculation:
- Current glucose: 180 mg/dL
- Target glucose: 120 mg/dL
- CIR: 1:15
- Correction factor: 1:50
- Meal carbs: 45g

Calculation: (45/15) + ((180-120)/50) = 3 + 1.2 = 4.2 units insulin (round to 4 units)

4. HYPERGLYCEMIA IN HOSPITALIZED PATIENTS

4.1 Significance of Inpatient Hyperglycemia
Connections to Outcomes:
- Hyperglycemia (> 180 mg/dL) associated with:
  * Impaired immune function
  * Increased infection risk
  * Increased cardiovascular events
  * Poor wound healing
  * Increased mortality (variable depending on underlying illness)
- Effect independent of prior diabetes diagnosis
- Often temporary (related to illness/stress)
- Gradual resolution as acute illness improves

4.2 Inpatient Glucose Target Goals

General Inpatients:
- Target: 140-180 mg/dL
- Avoids hypoglycemia risk while controlling hyperglycemia
- Reasonable balance for most patients

Critically Ill Patients:
- Target: 150-180 mg/dL (slightly higher to avoid hypoglycemia)
- Tight control (80-110) shown to increase hypoglycemia without major benefit
- Hypoglycemia itself associated with worse outcomes

Localized Infections/Sepsis:
- Target: < 180 mg/dL for infection control
- Benefits infection outcomes and mortality

Post-Operative Patients:
- Target: < 180 mg/dL
- Improves surgical site infection rates
- Reduces cardiac complications

4.3 Inpatient Insulin Protocols

Subcutaneous Basal-Bolus Regimen (Preferred):
For non-critical patients:
- Basal: Long-acting insulin dose (calculate from admission glucose if not on home insulin)
- Every 6 hours: Glucose checks
- Mealtime: Rapid-acting bolus insulin
- Bedtime: Additional intermediate or rapid-acting as needed
- Dosing algorithm: Insulin dose based on glucose + carb content

Trans-meningeal (Sliding Scale, Not Preferred):
- Outdated: Using only glucose to determine insulin without basal component
- Issues: Reactive (treats high glucose after already elevated), higher hyperglycemia rates
- Better as backup only between basal doses or for PO variability

Intravenous Insulin Infusion (ICU/Critical Illness):
Indications:
- Hyperglycemic crisis (DKA, HHS)
- Severe hyperglycemia in ICU patient
- Perioperative (especially cardiac surgery)
- Refractory hyperglycemia on SC insulin
- NPO status with prolonged illness
- Hemodynamic instability
- Expected significant fluctuations in glucose

Protocol:
- Infusion: Dilute 100 units regular insulin in 100 mL normal saline (1 unit/mL)
- Initial infusion rate: 2-5 units/hour (0.1 units/kg/hour typical)
- Glucose checks: Every 1 hour initially, then every 2-4 hours if stable
- Titration: Increase by 1-2 units/hour every 1-2 hours if glucose > 200
- Decrease by 1-2 units/hour if glucose < 100 or dropping rapidly
- Addition of dextrose: Add to IV if glucose < 100 mg/dL
- Transition: Switch to SC insulin once patient improving and stable

4.4 Transition from IV to Subcutaneous
When Patient Stable for Transition:
- Able to tolerate meals or tube feeding
- Hemodynamically stable
- No acute deterioration
- Declining insulin requirement (good sign)

Transition Protocol:
- Calculate total insulin used in last 24 hours (IV + boluses)
- New subcutaneous regimen: ~80% of previous IV daily requirement
- Basal: ~40-50% of new daily requirement (long-acting insulin)
- Boluses: ~50-60% divided among meals
- Continue IV insulin for 2 hours after first SC dose (allows insulin peaks)
- Check glucose 2-4 hours after transition
- Adjust SC doses based on glucose trends

5. DIABETIC KETOACIDOSIS (DKA)

5.1 Definition and Pathophysiology

Definition:
- Metabolic emergency characterized by:
  * Hyperglycemia (usually > 250 mg/dL)
  * Metabolic acidosis (pH < 7.3, bicarbonate < 15)
  * Ketonemia/ketonuria
  * Osmolality usually < 320 mOsm/kg (distinguishing from HHS)

Pathophysiology:
- Absolute or relative insulin deficiency
- Excess counterregulatory hormones (glucagon, catecholamines, cortisol, GH)
- Increased lipolysis: Free fatty acids → liver → ketone body production
- Ketones (acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetone): Overcome buffering capacity
- Severe metabolic acidosis: pH often 7.1-7.3 (sometimes < 7.0)
- Osmolality remain < 320 (vs HHS 320-350+)
- Kussmaul respirations: Compensatory hyperventilation

5.2 Precipitants of DKA

Infection:
- Most common precipitant (30-40% of cases)
- UTI, pneumonia, meningitis common sites
- Even asymptomatic bacteriuria can trigger

Diabetes Non-Compliance:
- New diagnosis or missed insulin doses
- Common in adolescents and young adults
- 20-30% of DKA cases

Acute Illness/Stress:
- Myocardial infarction
- Stroke
- Surgery/trauma
- Hyperthyroidism
- Increased counterregulatory hormones from illness stress

Insulin Pump Problems:
- Mechanical failure
- Infusion set disconnection
- Battery depletion
- Particularly rapid onset (within hours)

New Diagnosis:
- Type 1 diabetes presenting acutely
- 20-30% of T1DM diagnoses present as DKA
- Children, adolescents at high risk

Rarer Causes:
- Medications (SGLT2 inhibitors, atypical antipsychotics, corticosteroids)
- Pregnancy (gestational diabetes or pre-existing T1DM)
- Idiopathic (no clear precipitant identified)

5.3 Clinical Presentation

Symptoms:
- Polyuria, polydipsia (hours to days of history)
- Nausea and vomiting (often severe, may not be from GI pathology)
- Abdominal pain (may be severe, not always clear cause)
- Fatigue, weakness
- Headache, chest pain, altered mental status
- Rapid, deep breathing (Kussmaul respirations)
- Altered consciousness in severe cases

Physical Examination:
- Rapid, deep respirations (characteristic pattern)
- Fruity odor on breath (acetone)
- Dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor (dehydration)
- Hypotension (from significant fluid loss)
- Tachycardia
- Altered mental status, confusion, or coma (severe cases)
- Abdominal tenderness (pancreatic inflammation, DKA-related pain)

5.4 Diagnostic Evaluation

Laboratory Tests:
Plasma Glucose:
- Usually > 250 mg/dL
- Can be normal (euglycemic DKA) or very high (> 800 mg/dL)
- Check venous blood gas or serum pH

Arterial or Venous Blood Gas:
- pH < 7.35 (confirm metabolic acidosis)
- HCO3 < 15 mEq/L (low bicarbonate)
- Negative anion gap (actually metabolic acidosis component)
- PaCO2 low (compensatory hyperventilation, Kussmaul)

Ketones:
- Serum beta-hydroxybutyrate preferred (more accurate than urine ketones)
- > 3.0 mmol/L indicative of DKA
- Urine ketones positive (though can lag serum)
- Some labs now include beta-hydroxybutyrate in comprehensive metabolic panels

Electrolytes:
- Sodium: Often low (hyponatremia) or normal with artificial hyponatremia from hyperglycemia
- Potassium: Total body depleted BUT serum K+ often elevated initially (acidemia shifts K+ out of cells)
- Chloride: Low (hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis component)
- Calculated osmolality: Usually < 320 mOsm/kg (key distinguishing from HHS)

Anion Gap:
- Calculated: Na - (Cl + HCO3)
- Normal: 8-16 mEq/L
- In DKA: Elevated (> 15) due to ketone accumulation
- Serial measurement: Anion gap normalizes with treatment (ketones convert to neutral metabolites)

Common Laboratory Panel:
- CBC: WBC often elevated (stress response, infection)
- Creatinine: Often elevated (prerenal azotemia from dehydration)
- BUN elevated
- Amylase/lipase: May be mildly elevated (DKA can cause inflammatory pancreatitis)
- LFTs: Generally normal unless concurrent illness
- Urinalysis: Often shows ketones, glucose; pyuria if infection precipitant
- Lactate: Check for hyperlactatemia (worse prognosis)

5.5 Severity Assessment

Mild DKA:
- pH 7.25-7.30
- HCO3 15-18 mEq/L
- Alert or mildly confused
- Serum osmolality < 320

Moderate DKA:
- pH 7.15-<7.25
- HCO3 10-<15 mEq/L
- Alert or drowsy
- Moderate dehydration

Severe DKA:
- pH < 7.15
- HCO3 < 10 mEq/L
- Altered consciousness (stupor/coma)
- Severe dehydration
- Potential for multi-organ dysfunction

5.6 Treatment of DKA

Phase 1: Initial Stabilization and Assessment

1. Establish IV access:
- Two large-bore IV lines preferred
- Central line if severe hemodynamic compromise
- Labs drawn from initial IV: Glucose, BMP, pH/gas, ketones, CBC, lactate, ECG

2. Cardiac Monitoring:
- Continuous ECG monitoring (hyperkalemia risk)
- Monitor for peaked T-waves, widened QRS (hyperkalemia signs)

3. Oxygen Supplementation:
- Target SpO2 > 94%
- Don't over-oxygenate (avoid hyperoxia)
- Respiratory support if altered mental status (aspiration risk)

4. Diagnostic Confirmation:
- Blood glucose: Confirm hyperglycemia
- Serum electrolytes: Confirm hypokalemia, hyperkalemia, calculate anion gap
- Serum creatinine: Assess baseline renal function (impacts fluid replacement)
- Blood/urine cultures if infection suspected
- Pregnancy test if female of childbearing age
- Consider ECG changes suggesting acute MI or other acute illness
- Imaging only if high suspicion for other acute pathology

Phase 2: Fluid Resuscitation

Goals:
- Replace severe volume depletion (typically 5-10 liters lost)
- Restore intracellular and extracellular fluid
- Lower glucose gradually and appropriately
- Restore renal perfusion and urine output

Fluid Replacement Protocol:
- First hour: 1-1.5 L normal saline IV bolus (approximately 15 mL/kg)
- Subsequent hours:
  * Calculate deficit: (Current sodium X 0.6 X kg) - normal sodium (140)
  * Replace half deficit in first 8-12 hours, remainder in next 24 hours
  * Maintenance fluids: Add ~200-300 mL/hour for urine output and insensible losses
  * Example: 80 kg patient with Na 128: Deficit = (128 X 0.6 X 80) - (140 X 0.6 X 80) = 6144 - 6720 = 576 mEq from sodium depletion; fluid replacement ~4 liters over 24 hours
- Rate adjustment: Slow if cerebral edema suspected; faster if hypotensive or renal dysfunction
- NS infusion as glucose approaches 250 mg/dL  → switch to 5% dextrose with NS or KCl (to continue insulin infusion without hypoglycemia)
- Reassess volume status hourly: Vital signs, CVP if available, urine output goal > 0.5 mL/kg/hour
- Signs of adequate fluid replacement: Improved urine output, normalized BP, decreased resting HR

Phase 3: Potassium Replacement and Monitoring

Critical Point:
- Total body potassium depleted in ALL DKA cases (typically 5-10 mEq/kg loss)
- Serum K+ often HIGH initially (due to acidemia; K+ shifts out of cells)
- K+ MUST be repleted before or concurrent with insulin (insulin drives K+ into cells)
- Failure to repleted K+ can result in severe hypokalemia and cardiac arrhythmias

K+ Replacement Protocol:
Initial serum K+ < 3.5 mEq/L:
- Hold insulin; give potassium replacement FIRST
- KCl 20-40 mEq/L in IV fluids
- Recheck K+ after 1-2 hours of replacement before starting insulin
- Goal: Bring K+ to 4-5 mEq/L range before insulin

Initial serum K+ 3.5-5.0 mEq/L:
- Start potassium replacement: 20-30 mEq/L in IV fluids
- Can initiate insulin concurrently
- Recheck K+ every 2-4 hours
- Adjust K+ administration based on serum levels

Initial serum K+ > 5.5 mEq/L:
- Do NOT give potassium yet
- Proceed with insulin and fluids
- Monitor K+ closely (will drop significantly)
- Start K+ replacement when K+ < 5.5
- Check K+ every 1-2 hours even if hyperkalemic

Target potassium levels during treatment: 4-5 mEq/L

Phase 4: Insulin Infusion

Initiation:
- Start when K+ > 3.5 mEq/L (critical!)
- Delayed until K+ replacement if initially low
- Regular insulin IV infusion preferred (only IV-stable insulin)

Insulin Protocol:
- Preparation: 100 units regular insulin in 100 mL normal saline (1 unit/mL concentration)
- Initial infusion: 0.1 units/kg/hour (typical 5-10 units/hour)
- Example: 80 kg patient = 8 units/hour
- Can give IV bolus 10 units before infusion (optional; accelerates response)

Titration:
- Check glucose every 1 hour initially
- Increase infusion by 1-2 units/hour every 1-2 hours if glucose > 300 or not decreasing appropriately
- Target glucose decrease: 50-100 mg/dL per hour (~5-10% reduction)
- When glucose reaches 200-250 mg/dL:
  * Switch IV fluids to 5% dextrose with normal saline or LR
  * Maintain insulin infusion (don't stop abruptly)
  * Add potassium to fluids (20-40 mEq/L)
  * Reduce insulin rate if needed (goal: maintain glucose 150-200 mg/dL until acidosis resolved)

Transition to Subcutaneous:
- Start when:
  * pH > 7.30
  * HCO3 > 15-18 mEq/L (approaching normal)
  * Patient tolerating oral intake
  * Mild DKA or improving trend
- Start basal insulin (0.2-0.3 units/kg or patient's home dose if known)
- Continue IV insulin for 1-2 hours after first SC dose (overlapping coverage)
- Bolus insulin: 5-10 units with meals
- Adjust based on glucose trends

5.7 Monitoring and Reassessment During Treatment

Labs to Repeat:
- Serum glucose: Every 1 hour initially, every 2-4 hours once glucose stable
- Serum electrolytes (Na, K, Cl, HCO3): Every 2-4 hours initially
- Arterial or venous pH: Every 2-4 hours until pH > 7.30
- Serum beta-hydroxybutyrate or urine ketones: Can check to confirm resolution
- BUN/creatinine: Track renal function recovery

Vital Signs:
- Continuously monitor heart rate (watch for arrhythmias)
- Every 15-30 minutes: BP, RR
- Hourly: Temperature (watch for infection)
- Hourly: Urine output (goal > 0.5 mL/kg/hour)
- Mental status assessment

Labs Trending Toward Resolution:
- Glucose: 150-250 mg/dL
- pH: > 7.30
- HCO3: > 15-18 mEq/L
- K+: 4-5 mEq/L
- Anion gap normalizing
- Respiratory rate normalizing (less Kussmaul)
- Patient alert and oriented

5.8 Complications During Treatment

Cerebral Edema:
- Rare (< 1% of DKA cases but ~1% mortality from it)
- Risk factors: Young age, new T1DM diagnosis, severe acidosis, rapid treatment
- Symptoms: Altered mental status worsening, headache, bradycardia, hypertension, seizures
- Prevention: Slow fluid replacement in young/new diagnosis patients
- Treatment: Mannitol 0.5-1 g/kg IV, dexamethasone, restrict further fluid, mechanical ventilation if respiratory compromise

Arrhythmias:
- Related to: Hypokalemia (after K+ drops), hyperkalemia (initially), acidosis, cardiac ischemia
- Monitor: Continuous ECG
- Prevention: Appropriate K+ replacement
- Treatment: Standard ACLS algorithms

Rhabdomyolysis:
- Can occur in severe DKA
- Monitor: CK levels
- Treatment: Aggressive hydration, maintain urine output > 1 mL/kg/hour

Acute Kidney Injury:
- From dehydration and rhabdomyolysis
- Monitor: Creatinine trending, urine output
- May require dialysis if severe

Hypoglycemia:
- Risk during insulin infusion and SC transition
- Prevention: Glucose monitoring, adding dextrose to fluids when glucose < 250
- Treatment: IV dextrose, D50 amps, or juice if alert

5.9 Resolution of DKA

Discharged When:
- pH > 7.30
- HCO3 > 15 mEq/L (typical goal > 18)
- Anion gap normalized or near normal
- Oral intake tolerated
- Nausea/vomiting resolved
- Patient reliable for outpatient insulin administration
- Precipitant identified and managed (if possible)
- Follow-up arranged

Discharge Planning:
- Diabetes education (especially if new diagnosis)
- Insulin prescription: Injectable pens or vials
- Glucose monitoring supplies: Meters, strips, lancets
- Appointment with type 1 diabetes specialist or endocrinology
- Education on DKA warning signs and sick day management
- Supplies: Ketone testing (blood or urine), particularly for pumps users
- Psychiatric support if attempted medication omission
